# dump Brand Guidelines

## 1. Brand Overview

**Brand name:** dump  
**Category:** Digestive relief / release / regularity  
**Core idea:** Everyone does it. We make it easier.

**dump** is a blunt, modern digestive support brand designed to make an awkward, universal problem feel easier to talk about and easier to solve.

The brand is not built to be comedic or childish. It is built to be direct, distinctive, calming, useful, and memorable.

---

## 2. Brand Positioning

### Positioning statement
**dump** is the blunt, modern digestive relief brand that makes going easier — and talking about it less awkward.

### Brand purpose
To make digestive support feel clearer, more approachable, and less embarrassing.

### Brand mission
To help people feel relief, release, and regularity through products that are straightforward, effective, and easy to understand.

### Brand vision
To become the most recognisable modern brand for everyday digestive relief and regularity.

### Core brand promise
**Everyone does it. We make it easier.**

---

## 3. Brand Truth

The category is full of products that feel:
- cluttered
- overly clinical
- too soft or vague
- too colourful
- too wellness-coded
- too awkwardly euphemistic

**dump** stands out by saying the thing other brands avoid saying, but in a clean, confident, non-crass way.

This is not a joke brand.  
This is a **blunt, useful, modern relief brand**.

---

## 4. Brand Pillars

### Relief
Helping people feel lighter, more comfortable, and less weighed down.

### Release
Helping things move when they feel stuck.

### Regularity
Helping people build smoother, more predictable bathroom routines.

These three ideas should sit behind every product, claim, launch, and message.

## 6. Tone of Voice

### Tone principles
- Clear
- Direct
- Calm
- Human
- Slightly provocative
- Unashamed
- Minimal
- Mate-like, not manufactured

### What the tone should do
- reduce awkwardness
- remove fluff
- sound confident and modern
- make the product feel simple and trustworthy
- feel like a straight conversation, not ad-speak
- feel like you are talking to a mate, without becoming jokey

### What the tone should avoid
- toilet humour
- childish jokes
- gross language
- over-clever puns
- exaggerated detox language
- medical jargon unless necessary
- vague wellness speak
- “look how edgy we are” self-awareness

### Tone balance
The name **dump** already gives the brand its edge.

That means the copy does not need to constantly push harder. The strongest approach is:
- blunt enough to turn heads
- calm enough to build trust
- casual enough to feel human
- restrained enough to avoid becoming a novelty

### Good examples
- Everyone does it. We make it easier.
- Support for smoother mornings.
- Relief when you feel backed up.
- Daily support for regularity.
- Less awkward. More regular.
- Keep things moving.
- If you feel backed up, start here.
- Built for better bathroom days.

### Avoid examples
- explosive results
- flush the toxins
- bowel miracle
- mega cleanse
- poop jokes as the main brand voice
- anything that sounds like a stag-do T-shirt

## 7. Marketing Copy Approach

### Core rule
You can live on the edge, but you cannot become a cheesy joke brand.

The tone should feel like a straight, honest conversation with mates: blunt, easy, unembarrassed, and useful.

### What that means in practice
Marketing copy can be:
- bold
- head-turning
- slightly confrontational
- refreshingly plain-spoken
- culturally aware

But it should still feel:
- grown-up
- controlled
- clean
- trustworthy

### Copy hierarchy
Use different levels of boldness for different places.

#### High-boldness zones
These can push harder:
- ad headlines
- landing page hero lines
- social hooks
- email subject lines
- outdoor / flyer headlines

#### Medium-boldness zones
These should be clearer and steadier:
- product pages
- back-of-pack copy
- PDP bullets
- FAQs
- Amazon bullets

#### Low-boldness zones
These should prioritise trust and clarity:
- directions
- ingredients
- compliance copy
- health claims
- customer support copy

### Headline territory that fits
- Everyone does it. We make it easier.
- Feeling backed up?
- Let’s make this easier.
- Better mornings start here.
- A smoother way to go.
- Less awkward. More regular.
- Keep things moving.
- Daily support for when things feel stuck.

### Headline territory to be careful with
These may work sparingly, but should not define the brand:
- Need a dump?
- Backed up?
- Time to go.
- Unblock your morning.

### Headline territory to avoid
- Take a massive dump
- Drop the kids off
- unload your bowels
- joke-led toilet slang
- anything that feels crude for the sake of it

### CTA direction
CTAs can be more confident than most brands, but still clean.

Good CTA examples:
- Shop Daily Fibre
- Get things moving
- Start your routine
- Feel lighter
- Find your formula
- Make mornings easier

Use with care:
- Get relief now
- Start moving
- Unblock your routine

Avoid:
- Take a dump now
- dump today
- poo better
- anything that turns the product into a gag

## 9. Target Audience

### Primary audience
Adults aged **28–45** who experience occasional constipation, bloating, sluggish digestion, or irregularity, and are drawn to straightforward, modern brands that speak plainly and look better than traditional digestive products.

These are not people looking for toilet humour. They are people who are tired of awkward euphemisms, vague wellness language, and old-fashioned pharmacy-looking products.

### Secondary audience
Adults aged **45–60** who mainly care that the product works, but are still open to the blunt name if the packaging and messaging feel clean, credible, and not silly.

### Psychographic fit
The best-fit customer is more about attitude than age.

They are likely to:
- appreciate blunt honesty
- prefer modern, design-conscious brands
- want clarity over fluff
- be comfortable with slightly edgy language when it feels controlled
- want a product that feels effective without being over-medical
- feel that most digestive products either look boring, embarrassing, or overly “wellnessy”

### Problem states they recognise
- “I feel backed up.”
- “I don’t go properly.”
- “I feel bloated or heavy.”
- “Travel throws me off.”
- “I want a more regular routine.”
- “I want something that works, but I don’t want a weird old-person fibre brand.”

### Emotional drivers
They want:
- relief without embarrassment
- a product they can trust
- a brand that feels more honest and less awkward
- something simple to slot into real life
- a cleaner, more modern experience than the category usually offers

### Gender balance
The brand should be **unisex**, but may naturally skew slightly female at first because women often over-index in supplement and digestive-support purchasing.

That said, the visual identity and tone should stay neutral, confident, and broadly modern rather than feminine.

### Lower-fit audience
**dump** is likely to be a weaker fit for:
- very conservative shoppers who find the name too abrasive
- highly clinical shoppers who want a purely medical tone
- very soft wellness shoppers who prefer euphemistic language
- anyone looking for a joke or novelty product

### Likely emotional reaction to the brand
At first: “That’s bold.”  
Then: “Honestly, that’s exactly what this is for.”

### Practical audience shorthand
**dump** is for design-aware, bluntness-tolerant adults who have everyday digestive frustrations and are bored of soft, vague wellness brands.

## 12. Packaging Strategy

### Packaging objective
Stand out in a sea of sameness by being more restrained, more confident, and more visually calm than the category.

### Core packaging principle
**Confidence through simplicity.**

Most competitors are cluttered, text-heavy, overly colourful, or visually generic.  
**dump** should feel like the opposite.

### Front-of-pack approach
Minimal front-of-pack copy, with only the essentials:

**Daily Fibre**  
**dump**  
**Everyone does it. We make it easier.**

### What the front should communicate
1. what it is
2. the brand name
3. the brand promise

### What the front should not do
- over-explain the formula
- overload with claims
- include too many icons or badges
- feel noisy
- look medicinal or novelty-led

---

## 13. Packaging Design Direction

### Overall look
- single-colour pouch
- matte finish
- lots of negative space
- minimal text
- strong typography
- premium but accessible
- blunt but calm

### Visual character
**dump** should feel closer to a premium pantry, skincare, or design-led wellness product than a traditional digestive supplement.

### Desired reactions
- “That looks different.”
- “That feels premium.”
- “That looks simple and confident.”
- “It says exactly what it is without being embarrassing.”

---

## 14. Colour Direction

### Core recommendation
Use a **single-colour matte pouch** with restrained typography.

### Recommended colour family
A deep, design-led brown or taupe-brown rather than a bright or muddy brown.

### Good colour directions
- deep cocoa
- espresso
- dark taupe
- warm clay-brown
- mushroom-brown / greige-brown

### Avoid
- bright chocolate brown
- orange-brown
- muddy mid-brown
- kraft-paper cliché unless done very intentionally
- overly pastel wellness colours
- loud supplement-style colour combinations

### Why brown works
- subtle digestive association
- grounded and natural
- warm and mature
- understated but memorable

### Suggested system for future SKUs
Use a simple colour-block system by product while keeping the same minimal design language:
- Daily Fibre = deep brown / taupe-brown
- Debloat = muted sage
- Overnight Relief = deep plum / burgundy
- Travel Relief = slate blue-grey

The goal is consistency first, colour second.

## 16. Layout Principles

### Preferred layout
A clean stacked layout with generous spacing.

Example:

```text
Daily Fibre
dump
Everyone does it. We make it easier.
```

### Layout rules
- centre-aligned works well for the brand
- large amount of empty space is a feature, not a flaw
- avoid crowding the product with extra claims
- let **dump** carry the pack visually

### Design philosophy
The name is already the disruptive element.  
The design should not fight for attention as well.

The restraint is what makes it powerful.

---

## 17. Packaging Dos and Don'ts

### Do
- keep it stripped back
- prioritise whitespace
- make **dump** visually dominant
- keep the tone calm and confident
- make the front easy to read in seconds
- let the back of pack do the explanatory work

### Don't
- make it look like a joke product
- overload the front with selling points
- use too many textures, graphics, icons, or badges
- make it look rustic, crunchy, or outdated
- make it look like a medical pharmacy own-brand
- explain too much on the front

## 19. Founder Filter

When reviewing any creative, ask:

### Brand fit
- Does this feel blunt rather than funny?
- Does it make the problem feel less awkward?
- Does it feel premium and useful rather than novelty-led?
- Does it support relief, release, or regularity?

### Packaging fit
- Would this stand out in a cluttered supplement category?
- Is there enough restraint?
- Is the name doing the heavy lifting?
- Does it look trustworthy?
- Does it feel clean and confident at a glance?

### Copy fit
- Does this sound like a mate talking plainly, rather than a brand performing edge?
- Is it bold without becoming crude?
- Is the headline stronger than the body copy needs to be?
- Would this still feel premium if someone saw it on a shelf?
